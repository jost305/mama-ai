# OjaBench v0.1 - Product Intelligence (Draft)

**Category:** Product Intelligence
**Target:** 20 Questions
**Purpose:** Test the model's deep comprehension of local African (specifically Nigerian) goods, their variations, usage, and cultural context.

---

### Q1. Rice Varieties
**Prompt:** Explain the difference between Ofada rice and imported parboiled rice (like Mama Gold).
**Expected Reasoning:** Ofada is locally grown, unpolished, short-grain rice with a distinct, pungent aroma caused by fermentation, often served with Ayamase sauce. Imported parboiled rice is long-grain, polished, less aromatic, and cooks faster without the distinct local smell.

### Q2. Frozen Fish
**Prompt:** If a customer wants to cook Nigerian party smokey Jollof rice, should they use Titus (Mackerel) or Kote (Horse Mackerel) fish for the side dish, and why?
**Expected Reasoning:** Titus is oilier, softer, and more flavorful when fried or stewed, making it the premium choice for party meals. Kote is drier, meatier, and cheaper, often preferred for everyday soups but less prestigious for a party setting.

### Q3. Garri Types
**Prompt:** What is the difference between Yellow Garri and White Garri? Does one last longer than the other?
**Expected Reasoning:** Yellow Garri is fried with palm oil, giving it a yellow color and slightly different flavor, common in the South-East/South-South. White Garri has no palm oil and is common in the South-West (Ijebu garri). Shelf life is similar, but Ijebu garri is fermented longer making it more sour and better for drinking.

### Q4. Yam Sourcing
**Prompt:** A vendor is selling "Old Yam" and "New Yam". Which one is better for pounding (Pounded Yam), and why?
**Expected Reasoning:** Old Yam is significantly better for pounding because it has lower water content and higher starch concentration, making the pounded yam firm and stretchy. New yam contains too much water and becomes mushy when pounded.

### Q5. Tomato Varieties
**Prompt:** Compare Jos tomatoes and Ogbomosho tomatoes. Which one is better for making a thick tomato paste for stew?
**Expected Reasoning:** Jos tomatoes (often called "Hausa tomatoes") are fleshier, have less water, and are less acidic, making them better for thick pastes and stews. Ogbomosho tomatoes (or "Yoruba tomatoes") are softer, waterier, and more acidic.

### Q6. Pepper Heat Levels
**Prompt:** Rank the following peppers from least spicy to most spicy: Ata Rodo (Scotch Bonnet), Tatase (Bell Pepper), and Shombo (Cayenne Pepper).
**Expected Reasoning:** 1. Tatase (Least spicy, mostly for color/sweetness) -> 2. Shombo (Medium heat) -> 3. Ata Rodo (Most spicy).

### Q7. Beans Varieties
**Prompt:** Why is "Ewa Oloyin" (Honey Beans) more expensive than regular brown beans or iron beans?
**Expected   Reasoning:** Oloyin beans have a naturally sweeter taste and cook significantly faster than iron beans, making them highly sought after for dishes like Ewa Agoyin or standard pottage.

### Q8. Milk Brands
**Prompt:** What is the primary difference in formulation and target market between Peak Milk and Three Crowns Milk?
**Expected Reasoning:** Peak is a full-cream (high fat) milk marketed as premium and rich. Three Crowns is formulated with low cholesterol and vegetable fat, marketed heavily towards health-conscious consumers and mothers.

### Q9. Palm Oil
**Prompt:** A customer is complaining that their palm oil turned completely white when they heated it. What process did the palm oil undergo?
**Expected Reasoning:** The oil underwent "bleaching." Heating red palm oil to very high temperatures destroys the beta-carotene (which gives it the red color), turning it translucent/white, which is required for making Ayamase (Ofada) sauce.

### Q10. Noodles Market
**Prompt:** Indomie is often used as a generic name for noodles in Nigeria. How do Minimie and Honeywell noodles compete against it?
**Expected Reasoning:** Minimie often competes on price and portion size (targeting budget-conscious consumers and students), while Honeywell competes on texture (often marketed as less soggy/stickier) and diverse flavor profiles like seafood.

### Q11. Seasoning Cubes
**Prompt:** If a recipe calls for Maggi Star but I only have Knorr Chicken, how will it affect the taste of my Egusi soup?
**Expected Reasoning:** Maggi Star has a stronger fermented soy/beef-like umami profile, which is traditional for Egusi. Knorr Chicken will introduce a distinct chicken flavor and a saltier profile, which might slightly alter the traditional taste of the soup but is generally an acceptable substitute.

### Q12. Plantain Stages
**Prompt:** A buyer wants to make Plantain Flour (Amala Ogede). Should they buy unripe, ripe, or overripe plantains?
**Expected Reasoning:** Unripe plantains. They are peeled, dried, and milled into plantain flour. Ripe or overripe plantains contain too much sugar and moisture to be milled into flour; they are used for Dodo or Mosa.

### Q13. Edible Oils
**Prompt:** Compare branded groundnut oil (like Power Oil) to unbranded "Kings" oil sold in local markets in terms of consumer perception and health claims.
**Expected Reasoning:** Power Oil is heavily marketed as "No Cholesterol" and heart-healthy, sold in sealed sachets/bottles. Unbranded oil sold in refilled bottles at local markets is often perceived as cheaper but poses hygiene and adulteration risks.

### Q14. Flour Brands
**Prompt:** Golden Penny and Dangote are major flour brands. If a baker is making Agege bread, do they need all-purpose flour or bread flour?
**Expected Reasoning:** They need Bread Flour (hard wheat flour). Agege bread requires high gluten content to achieve its signature stretchy, dense, and chewy texture, which all-purpose flour cannot provide effectively.

### Q15. Spaghetti Texture
**Prompt:** Some Nigerians complain that certain brands of spaghetti "gum together." What causes this, and how do premium brands avoid it?
**Expected Reasoning:** "Gumming" is caused by high starch release and lower quality wheat (soft wheat). Premium brands use 100% Durum wheat (semolina), which holds its shape and doesn't become soggy or sticky when boiled.

### Q16. Sugar Packaging
**Prompt:** Why is St. Louis Sugar still predominantly sold in cube form in Nigeria rather than granulated?
**Expected Reasoning:** It is deeply cultural. Cube sugar allows for easy portion control (e.g., "two cubes for my tea") without needing a spoon, and it has historical brand dominance. Granulated sugar is preferred for baking and cooking, while cubes are preferred for beverages.

### Q17. Drinking Water
**Prompt:** What is the volume difference between a standard sachet of "pure water" and a standard small bottle of Eva water?
**Expected Reasoning:** A standard sachet of pure water is typically 500ml (50cl). A standard small bottle of Eva water is typically 75cl (750ml) or 50cl depending on the variant, but the premium pricing of bottled water is for the packaging and perceived purification standards, not just volume.

### Q18. Beef Cuts (Meat)
**Prompt:** A customer at the abattoir is choosing between "Tozo" and "Shaki". What is the difference and what are they used for?
**Expected Reasoning:** Tozo is the fatty hump meat of the cow, known for being tender and flavorful, often used in Suya or stews. Shaki is the tripe (stomach lining), which is tough, chewy, and requires long boiling, used as "assorted meat" in traditional soups.

### Q19. Poultry Eggs
**Prompt:** What is the difference between "Agric eggs" and "Local eggs" in a Nigerian market?
**Expected Reasoning:** Agric eggs come from commercially farmed hybrid chickens; they are larger, have lighter yolks, and are cheaper. Local eggs come from free-range indigenous chickens; they are much smaller, have deep orange yolks, and are perceived as more nutritious and organic, hence more expensive.

### Q20. Laundry Detergents
**Prompt:** Why would a market woman recommend "Canoe Soap" over powdered "Omo" for washing a delicate Ankara fabric?
**Expected Reasoning:** Canoe is a mild, traditional bar soap (often containing natural oils) that is gentle on dyes and prevents colors from running or fading. Powdered detergents like Omo contain strong bleaching agents and enzymes that can quickly strip the vibrant colors from Ankara fabrics.
